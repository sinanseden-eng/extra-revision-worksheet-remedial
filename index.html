The De-Extinction Debate

For decades, the idea of bringing extinct animals back to life belonged entirely to science fiction. However, rapid advancements in genetic engineering have turned this fantasy into a very real possibility. Scientists are currently working on projects to resurrect species like the woolly mammoth and the passenger pigeon by extracting preserved DNA from museum artifacts and frozen remains. Supporters of "de-extinction" argue that humans have a moral obligation to fix the damage we have done to the planet, claiming that returning these animals to the wild could restore broken ecosystems.

Despite the excitement surrounding this groundbreaking technology, many conservationists are deeply alarmed. Critics argue that modern environments have adapted since these animals disappeared. Reintroducing a mammoth to a 21st-century landscape could result in the creature acting as an invasive species, potentially destroying the food sources of native wildlife. Furthermore, opponents highlight a major financial concern: de-extinction requires billions of dollars. They firmly believe that this massive funding should be directed toward protecting the thousands of living species that are currently on the edge of extinction, rather than obsessing over animals that died out thousands of years ago.

1. According to the text, what is the primary environmental concern critics have about de-extinction?

2. How do opponents of the project view the financial aspect of bringing back extinct animals?
